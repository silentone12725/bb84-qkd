The Problem

Modern internet security relies on algorithms like RSA and ECC, whose strength comes from the computational difficulty of factoring large numbers — a task that takes classical computers millions of years at scale.

Shor's quantum algorithm breaks this entirely, factoring in polynomial time. As quantum hardware matures, every RSA-encrypted message — including those recorded today — becomes decryptable. This is the "harvest now, decrypt later" attack.

The fundamental gap: classical cryptography cannot detect a passive eavesdropper. An adversary can silently copy every transmitted bit, leaving no trace whatsoever.

The Solution — BB84 Protocol

BB84 (Bennett & Brassard, 1984) replaces mathematical hardness with laws of physics. Security is information-theoretic — unbreakable even with unlimited compute.

Alice encodes each bit as a qubit in a randomly chosen basis: Z-basis (|0⟩, |1⟩) or X-basis (|+⟩, |−⟩). Bob measures randomly. They compare bases publicly, discard mismatches, and the remaining bits form the shared secret key.

The trap for Eve: the No-Cloning Theorem forbids copying an unknown quantum state. Eve must guess the basis — wrong 50% of the time — and every wrong guess irreversibly disturbs the qubit, raising the QBER to ~25%. If QBER > 11%, the key is discarded and Eve is exposed.

P(error | Eve) = P(Eve wrong basis) × P(Bob error | Eve wrong)
               = 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.25 → 25% QBER
